## Featured Projects

### B9 Robot - New Zealand's Only AI-Powered B9 Environmental Control Robot
[https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/b9-robot-mobile-laboratory.html](https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/b9-robot-mobile-laboratory.html)

A fully working, AI-powered child-friendly likeness of the B9 Environmental Control Robot from the classic TV series *Lost in Space*, built by Michael Fenton in New Zealand. This unique interactive robot is used as a mobile science laboratory and STEM engagement tool for learners of all ages. It features embedded AI, voice control, real-time sensor data broadcasting, and support for the RIGEL application. The robot can speak te reo Maori and other languages, and is designed for classroom resilience and letting children see themselves as scientists. This is believed to be the only standalone AI-enabled B9 robot in New Zealand. Michael has composed 'Roll with me B9' music videos suitable for young and older learners, showcasing the art, creativity and fun of authentic hands-on science and maths.

### Casio Calculator Ultra Low Cost Data Logger - Using Picaxe, Micro:bit, or ESP32
[https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/casio-calculator-data-logger-hack.html](https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/casio-calculator-data-logger-hack.html)

Michael Fenton pioneered timed-interval data logging using Casio FX-9750, FX-9860, and FX-CG50 calculators connected to low-cost Picaxe sensor units. He discovered READY-gated timing windows in the Casio serial communication protocol to pause RECEIVE() without a COM error. Ultra low cost timed sensor recording using common school graphing calculators. Project-based learning resources scaffold science, mathematics, and data literacy in schools world-wide.

Simultaneous synchronous multi-sensor transmission paper in preparation. SHA-256 hash of manuscript in preparation, March 2026: [6F0C9402CD3F0715F3660BC864322569D40205D0C96F509097A62C10A98B0B3A]

### RIGEL - Real-World Interactive Games and Electronics Link
[https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/rigel-games-electronics-link.html](https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/rigel-games-electronics-link.html)

Michael Fenton invented RIGEL, a universal real-world interface that connects low-cost DIY sensors and microcontrollers to game design software, turning a game engine into a fully functional science lab, data logger, and industrial measurement and control system. RIGEL auto-connects and recognises sensors on Picaxe, Arduino, ESP8266, and ESP32 boards. Sensors can measure temperature, pressure, UV light, infrared, magnetic fields, salinity, alpha radiation, and more, many costing just a few cents. RIGEL can speak te reo Maori and other languages, control robots remotely, simulate CPR training, and allow students to fly a 3D flight simulator using an exercise bike as the controller.

### RIGEL-WEB - The Browser Port of RIGEL
[https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/rigel-web.html](https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/rigel-web.html)

A free, self-contained single-file HTML port of RIGEL. It connects low-cost DIY sensors, wearable sensors, and microcontrollers (Picaxe, Arduino, ESP8266, ESP32, BBC Micro:bit) via USB-serial, Bluetooth, or WiFi for real-time data logging, life-function monitoring, and game control. Modules include Science Lab, Home Security, CPR Simulator, 2D Sensor Game, 3D Flight Simulator, Robot Remote Control, and Industrial Measurement and Control. It runs offline in Chromium-based browsers with no installation. Zenodo DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.20541487.

### SMART - The Sound-to-Motion Analysis and Recording Tool
[https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/smart.html](https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/smart.html)

A free, self-contained single-file HTML science instrument that turns the audio system in any school device into a precision event timer (theoretical resolution about 21 to 23 microseconds) and a waveform and spectrum scope. Six module families cover capture and calibration, linear motion and gravity, periodic motion, rotational motion, two-event timing, and resonance and frequency, with a built-in simulator so every experiment works without hardware. It runs in Chrome, Edge, Firefox, and Safari. Zenodo DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.20542011.

### Digital Multimeters as Science Data Loggers
[https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/digital-multimeters-as-data-loggers.html](https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/digital-multimeters-as-data-loggers.html)

Michael pioneered the use of digital multimeters (DMM) as inexpensive science data logger substitutes in New Zealand primary and secondary classrooms. Home-made sensors costing just a few cents can detect a wide range of environmental factors, enabling school and home-based authentic hands-on inquiry learning in science and maths.

### Game Design and Coding for Cross-Curricula STEM Learning
[https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/game-design-coding-learning.html](https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/game-design-coding-learning.html)

Michael Fenton uses accessible game design software as a vehicle for integrated STEM, arts, and music learning in New Zealand schools. His "Build it, Test it, Use it" pedagogy uses block-based and GML coding to create games that incorporate real-world sensor data, voice acting, original soundtracks, and graphic design. Classroom examples include DNA electrophoresis simulators, 3D TARDIS games, CPR timing simulators, and a 2025 remake of the classic 1982 Apple II game Transylvania, complete with an original AI-assisted soundtrack. Game design naturally integrates mathematics, physics, coding, performing arts, and music into single learner-centred projects, supporting low-floor, high-ceiling differentiated learning for all ability levels.

### Maker Science - Learning Science Through Making, Robotics, and Electronics
[https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/maker-science-robotics-electronics.html](https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/maker-science-robotics-electronics.html)

Michael Fenton's Maker Science pedagogy transforms abstract STEM concepts into tangible, working devices that students build, test, and use to investigate the world. Rooted in constructionist learning and inquiry-based practice, the approach uses low-cost Picaxe microcontrollers, breadboards, and DIY sensors to make sophisticated projects accessible in any New Zealand school regardless of budget. The Input-Process-Output framework connects electronics, biology, physics, and computer science naturally, and the open-source resources and YouTube documentation share these methods with educators globally.

### Artificial Intelligence in Education
[https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/artificial-intelligence-in-education.html](https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/artificial-intelligence-in-education.html)

Analysis of current AI use in education, including the cognitive risks raised by recent research, the warning that AI models trained on a contaminated scientific literature cannot stand in for real measurement, and the opportunity of using AI as a creative and technical partner when the thinking stays with the learner.

[https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/science-is-not-everywhere-but-it-can-begin-anywhere.html](https://mikefentonnz.github.io/projects/science-is-not-everywhere-but-it-can-begin-anywhere.html)

## Research Record (1994 to 2026)

A connected body of practice-embedded research is openly archived on Zenodo with permanent DOI identifiers, spanning molecular microbiology, low-cost science instrumentation, and STEM education. The full, citable list is on the [Research Outputs and Publications](https://mikefentonnz.github.io/research-outputs-publications-portfolio.html) page, alongside the complete peer-reviewed and conference record.

The root of this body of work is a 1994 Masters thesis in microbiology at Massey University, research that established Michael's identity as a producer of scientific knowledge rather than a deliverer of it. Professor David Penny (Emeritus Distinguished Professor CNZM FRSNZ) later described the distinction precisely: a science teacher delivers established knowledge; a teacher of science produces knowledge in the mode of science, even when teaching.

That disciplinary identity made possible the founding of the Nexus Research Group in 1997, New Zealand's only secondary school-based research laboratory, through which the tools and culture of authentic open science were brought into the compulsory education system. The works that follow, in sensor instrumentation, teacher professional development, low-cost data logging, and curriculum design, are the sustained record of what that founding made possible.

## Theoretical Framework: Design by Subversion

A constraint-driven framework, [Design by Subversion](https://mikefentonnz.github.io/design-by-subversion-stem-framework.html), has been identified retrospectively from this 35-year body of work. It describes how educators turn hard constraints into the design brief by exploiting the unintended affordances of existing tools, systems, and institutions, across technical, pedagogical, and institutional strands. It is a working framework, not yet formally peer-reviewed, and is being prepared for submission as part of a PhD by publication.

## About Michael Fenton

Michael Fenton (MRSNZ) is one of New Zealand's most broadly qualified and experienced STEM educators, with a career spanning all three science disciplines, biology, chemistry, and physics, as well as mathematics including calculus, taught from primary to tertiary and polytechnic level. He is also an expert in digital technology, robotics, electronics, and game design as educational tools, and an experienced STEM course designer, education researcher, and assessment expert. With decades of combined research, industry, and teaching experience, he is among a very small number of New Zealand educators who combine deep subject knowledge across all sciences and mathematics with hands-on expertise in modern digital technology pedagogy.

He founded New Zealand's only secondary school-based research group, the Nexus Research Group, whose patron was Dr William 'Bill' Pickering, former head of NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL). The group encouraged students of all ages to conduct authentic citizen science research, presented at national conferences and provided to the Ministry of Education.

Michael received a Ministry of Education e-Learning Fellowship for his pioneering work on low-cost digital technology tools for science and mathematics education. He has designed and delivered NZQA-approved Level 7 in-service teacher professional development qualifications in science and mathematics teaching, mentoring teachers and principals across New Zealand.

He has been a keynote speaker at conferences including SciCon, and his work has been featured in newspapers, radio, television, and education journals. He co-designed (with Dr Christine Fenton) New Zealand's first approved alternative to NCEA, a New Zealand Certificate in Study and Employment Pathways (Level 3 and Level 4) for Green School New Zealand, accredited by NZQA and meeting University Entrance requirements. His philosophy of teaching to interests and abilities rather than age group has influenced educators and curriculum designers across the compulsory and vocational education sectors.

Michael builds interactive science fiction props including a TARDIS, a Dalek, a Ghostbusters proton pack, and New Zealand's only AI-powered B9 Environmental Control Robot, all used as authentic STEM engagement tools.
